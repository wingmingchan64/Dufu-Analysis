Yes — that distinction is very important, and I think you are right to make it explicit now.

What you are calling comparing and mixing are not two versions of the same operation. They are structurally different.

1. Comparing vs mixing
Comparing

Comparing means:

units from different editions are aligned
only units that can be meaningfully aligned should be shown together
the view depends on structural correspondence

So comparison is not just accumulation. It requires a common frame.

Mixing

Mixing means:

materials from different editions are simply gathered together
they may be assigned to the same anchor
no strong claim of one-to-one correspondence is implied

So mixing is closer to aggregation.

That is a very useful distinction. Once it is clear, many later decisions become easier.

2. Why smaller anchors disappear in comparison

Your thought here seems sound:

when listing/comparing editions, many anchors will be gone

Yes. Because fine-grained anchors depend on local editorial structure, and that structure may not align across editions.

For example:

one edition has 夾注 after a character
another has no corresponding local note
one edition breaks things differently
one inline note corresponds only loosely to another note placed elsewhere

At that level, alignment becomes weak or artificial.

So I agree that in comparison views, the anchor system should probably contract upward.

What survives most naturally are:

行錨
段錨
詩錨
組詩 / 文檔層 anchors

These correspond to units that are more likely to align across editions.

That feels right.

3. Why line is a natural lower bound

Your intuition that “anything smaller than lines probably cannot be compared” makes good practical sense.

Not because it is metaphysically impossible, but because below the line level:

editorial placement varies too much
annotations may target different spans
commentarial granularity diverges
punctuation and inline formatting differ

Once you go down to 句 or 字, you are no longer merely comparing edition content; you are already comparing local editorial behavior.

That may still be possible in special cases, but it is no longer a general comparison framework.

So treating line as the usual lowest common comparison unit is a very good working rule.

4. This leads to a useful principle

You could almost say:

comparison uses only anchors belonging to a shared alignment layer

That shared layer will usually be:

line and above

Whereas:

char anchors
segment anchors
many fine ornaments

remain edition-internal.

That is a strong and elegant distinction.

5. Assignment of combined texts

You also said:

An anchor can of course be assigned combined texts from different editions.

Yes, and that fits perfectly with the architecture you’ve built.

Because once assignment is separated from rendering, the anchor itself does not care whether it receives:

one text
several texts
a structured comparison object
a mixed aggregate

That is excellent.

It means anchors remain stable, while the payload can vary by view.

So a line anchor might receive:

in a single-edition view: one annotation block
in a comparison view: aligned annotations from several editions
in a mixed view: an unordered or loosely ordered bundle

This is exactly the kind of flexibility your assignment/rendering split was meant to achieve.

6. A possible conceptual distinction

You may later find it helpful to distinguish two kinds of assigned payload:

Aligned payload

For comparison:

edition A ↔ edition B ↔ edition C
structurally matched
Aggregated payload

For mixing:

collected under one anchor
not necessarily aligned internally

You do not need formal terms yet, but the distinction is already there.

7. The larger gain

What I especially like is that your system now allows you to say:

the tree stays the same
the available anchors are known
the assignment policy changes by task
the rendering policy changes by task

So “single edition,” “comparison,” and “mixing” are no longer three different architectures. They are three different uses of the same architecture.

That is a major achievement.
