AI said:
Yes — that distinction is very important, and I think you are right to separate the two.

What you are now calling comparison and mixing are not two variants of the same thing. They are structurally different operations.

1. Comparison requires alignment

When editions are compared, the system must be able to say:

these two things correspond
these two things differ
this note belongs to the same unit in both editions
this note has no counterpart in the other edition

So comparison presupposes some shared unit of reference. In your present architecture, that naturally means:

line anchors
poem anchors
group-poem anchors
perhaps paragraph anchors, where paragraphing is truly comparable

But below the line level, things become unstable very quickly:

segmentation may differ
punctuation may differ
note placement may differ
one edition may have no corresponding local anchor at all

So your instinct is sound:

for comparison, anchors smaller than line level will usually drop out

Not because they are useless in themselves, but because they do not survive cross-edition alignment well enough.

2. Mixing does not require alignment

What you call “mixing” is different. There, the question is not:

what corresponds to what?

but rather:

what materials do I want to gather here?

So yes, in that case an anchor can receive:

text from edition A
text from edition B
text from edition C

without any claim that they are aligned internally.

This is much looser. It is aggregation, not comparison.

So I would say:

comparison = alignment-based
mixing = collection-based

That distinction is excellent, and I think it will save you a lot of confusion later.

3. This also clarifies which anchors are “portable” across editions

You are beginning to see that some anchors are structurally robust enough to survive edition comparison, and some are not.

Likely portable
line anchors
poem anchors
group/document anchors
perhaps some paragraph anchors, if the edition tradition supports them
Likely not portable
char anchors
many segment anchors
small local hooks tied to edition-specific annotation style

That is a very useful distinction. It means you do not have to force one uniform anchor system into every use case.

Instead, you can say:

some anchors are intra-edition anchors

some anchors are inter-edition comparable anchors

That seems truer to the materials.

4. A very helpful consequence

Once you accept this, comparison stops being “compare everything.” It becomes:

compare only what can be aligned without distortion

That is a mature rule.

Otherwise one is tempted to compare at too fine a level simply because the data exists. But in textual work, finer is not always better. Sometimes finer means false precision.

Your line-level judgment is therefore not merely pragmatic; it is methodologically sound.

5. Assignment can still stay unified

The nice thing is that your anchor-assignment model still works.

An anchor may be assigned:

one edition’s text
several editions’ texts
a merged display object
a comparison bundle

So the assignment mechanism itself need not split into two completely different systems.

The difference lies in what is being assigned.

For example:

in a single-edition view, a line anchor receives one note block
in a mixed view, a line anchor receives several note blocks
in a comparison view, a line anchor receives an aligned comparison object

That is elegant, because it keeps the tree stable and lets the semantics of the payload vary.

6. I would phrase the distinction like this

You may eventually want language along these lines:

Comparison requires alignment of corresponding units across editions and therefore operates only on anchors that are structurally comparable.
Mixing does not require alignment; it only aggregates materials from multiple editions under a shared anchor.

That feels very close to what you just said.

7. One further implication

This may also mean that your system will eventually need two kinds of “combined payload”:

Mixed payload

A bag or list of materials from different editions, without claiming detailed correspondence.

Comparative payload

A structured object whose parts are explicitly aligned across editions.

That distinction belongs in the metadata/rendering layer, not in the tree. Which is good. The tree should not have to care.

8. My overall judgment

This is a strong clarification.

You are no longer just thinking “multiple editions” in the abstract. You are distinguishing:

what can be aligned
what can only be gathered
what anchor levels survive comparison
what anchor levels remain edition-local

That will make your later work much steadier.

And yes, I think your present conclusion is right:

for real comparison, line anchors and larger units are the natural core; anything smaller is usually too fragile or too edition-specific.

That is not a limitation of the system. It is respect for the nature of the texts.
